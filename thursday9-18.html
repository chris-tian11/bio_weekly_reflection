Structure and Functions of Carbs, Lipids, Proteins, and Nucleic Acids

(Notes on textbook pages 52-57)

  • Why is carbon so important?:
    • Carbon atoms have 4 valence electrons, allowing it to form strong covalent bonds with many other elements
    • 1 carbon atom can bond to another, which gives carbon the ability to form chains that can almost span to an infinite length
  • No other element matches carbon's versatility and ability to build
  • The large organic molecules found in living things are known as macromolecules (giant moleculte)
  • Macromolecules are created from monomers which join together to create polymers
  • 4 major groups of macromolecules are found in living things
    • Carbohydrates
      • Ex: sugar, starch, and cellulose
      • Made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms, usually in a ratio of 1:2:1
      • The breakdown of sugars apply immediate energy for the cell
      • Many organisms store extra sugar as complex carbohydrates known as starch
      • Simple Sugars:
        • Simple sugar molecules area slo known as monosaccharides
        • Besides glucose, monosaccharides include galactose and fructose
        • A disaccharide is a compound made by joining together 2 simple sugars, fructose and glucose
      • Complex Carbohydrates:
        • The macromolecules formed by joining many monosaccharides together is called a polysaccharide
        • Many animals store excess sugar in a polysaccharide called glycogen which is used to supply energy for muscle contraction
      • Starches and Cellulose:
        • Plants use a different polysaccharide to store excess sugar called starch
        • Plants make an important polysaccharide called cellulose
        • Cellulose fibers give plants their strength and rigidity
        • Cellulose is the major component of paper and wood
    • Lipids
      • Large and varied group of macromolecules
      • Not soluble in water
      • Made mostly from carbon and hydrogen atoms
      • Lipids include the compounds we call fats, oils, and waxes
      • Lipids can be used to store energry, and they form important parts of biological membranes and waterproof coverings
      • Many lipids form when glycerol combines with compounds called fatty acids
    • Nucleic Acids
      • Nucleotides are monomers that consist of three components:
        • 5-carbon sugar
        • Phosphate group
        • Nitrogenous base
      • Nucleic acids are polymers assembled from nucleotides
      • Nucleoides can be joined together by covalent bonds to form a polynucleotide or a nucleic acid
      • There are 2 types of nucleic acids:
        • DNA
          • Contains the sugar deoxyribose
        • RNA
          • Contains the sugar ribose
    • Proteins
      • Contains nitrogens, carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen
      • Proteins are monomers of molecule called amino acids
      • Amino acids are compounds with an amino group on one end and a carboxyl group on the other
